01 — The Weird Idea

What if you just… threw stuff?

Let's say you forgot the formula for the area of a circle. No problem. Just draw a square, put a circle inside it, throw lots of random dots at the square, and count how many land inside the circle.

That's it. That number can tell you the area of the circle — and even the famous number π (3.14…). No magic. Just counting.

"The circle takes up some fraction of the square. Count the dots. Do the math. Done."

If you throw 100 dots and 78 land in the circle, that's 78%. The circle's area is about 78% of the square's area. Since you know the square's area (length × width), you now know the circle's area — and from that, you can calculate π.

02 — The Origin Story

The Man Sick in Bed

A scientist named Stan Ulam was sick in bed, playing cards. He wondered: what's the chance I'll win this game? Instead of doing hard math, he thought — why not just play the game millions of times in my head and count wins and losses?

That's exactly what Monte Carlo simulation does. Repeat something random many, many times and see what happens. They named it after a casino in Monaco called Monte Carlo — because it's all about chance.

03 — Does It Work?

More darts = better answer

Kinda. But more dots means a better answer. Here's the catch: to get twice as accurate, you need four times more dots. So it's slow — but it works.

Darts Thrown Accuracy How Close?
100 ~2 decimal places Close enough
1,000 ~3 decimal places Getting better
10,000 ~4 decimal places Pretty good
1,000,000 ~5–6 decimal places Really, really good

This method works even for super hard problems — like shapes in 100 dimensions (don't worry, nobody can picture those either). Normal math gives up. Monte Carlo just throws more dots.
